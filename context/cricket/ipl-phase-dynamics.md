# IPL Phase Dynamics — Statistical Patterns and Tactical Norms

This file documents the structural patterns of T20 innings in the IPL,
used when writing team-analysis.md and prediction.md.

---

## Phase 1: Powerplay (Overs 1–6)

### Field restrictions

Only two fielders allowed outside the 30-yard circle. This creates
gaps in the outfield and incentivises aggressive batting.

### Typical scoring patterns

- **Tournament-average run rate:** 7.5–8.5 RPO across recent IPL seasons
- **Typical powerplay score:** 45–55 runs
- **Above average:** 55+ (strong platform or aggressive intent)
- **Below average:** Below 40 (early wickets or conservative approach)
- **Exceptional:** 65+ (explosive opening stand)

### Wicket patterns

- Average wickets lost in powerplay: 1.2–1.5
- Teams losing 0–1 wickets in the powerplay go on to post 175+ roughly
  65% of the time.
- Teams losing 3+ wickets in the powerplay post 175+ only ~20% of the
  time.
- The powerplay is the phase where early wickets have the most
  disproportionate effect on the final total.

### Player types that dominate

- **Batters:** Aggressive openers who target the field restrictions.
  Strike rate in powerplay is the single most important batting metric
  for openers.
- **Bowlers:** New-ball specialists who swing or seam the ball. Left-arm
  pace is particularly effective in the powerplay due to the angle
  across right-handed openers. Express pace (145+) takes wickets through
  bounce and pace. Powerplay economy below 7.0 is elite.

### Common tactics

- **Batting:** Most teams now attack from ball one. The "see off the new
  ball" approach is largely extinct in the IPL — data shows that
  conservative powerplays reduce final totals by 15–25 runs on average
  without significantly reducing wicket risk.
- **Bowling:** Two strike bowlers in the powerplay (typically the best
  seamer and one support seamer). Spinner in the powerplay is
  increasingly common (especially wrist spin) when conditions suit.
  Field is aggressive: slips, gully, short cover.

### Prediction implications

A powerplay score 15+ runs above or below the venue average is a
strong signal about the final total. Adjust first-innings total
estimate proportionally.

## Phase 2: Middle Overs (Overs 7–15)

### Field restrictions

Maximum five fielders outside the 30-yard circle. This is the phase
where captains have maximum tactical flexibility.

### Typical scoring patterns

- **Tournament-average run rate:** 8.0–9.0 RPO (has been increasing
  in recent seasons due to Impact Player rule and aggressive batting
  evolution)
- **Typical middle-over runs:** 72–81 runs (over 9 overs)
- **Above average:** 85+ (sustained acceleration or weak bowling)
- **Below average:** Below 65 (spin choke or wicket cluster)

### Wicket patterns

- Average wickets lost: 2.0–3.0 in this phase
- The "middle-over squeeze" — when a bowling team takes 2–3 wickets
  while conceding only 65–70 runs — is often the decisive passage of
  the match.
- Wicket clusters in overs 8–12 are particularly damaging because they
  bring in lower-middle-order batters who are not yet set.

### Player types that dominate

- **Batters:** Versatile middle-order players who can both accumulate
  and accelerate. The No. 3 batter's role is to bridge the powerplay
  aggression with death-over finishing. Strike rotation (singles and
  twos) is more important than boundaries in overs 7–12.
- **Bowlers:** Spinners are at peak influence. Off-spinners and
  leg-spinners dominate overs 7–15 in terms of wickets per over.
  Left-arm orthodox bowlers have excellent economy rates in this phase.
  The captain's ability to use spin effectively is a key differentiator.

### Common tactics

- **Batting:** Most teams aim for 8.0+ RPO minimum. The "set batter
  accelerates while a new batter rotates strike" pattern is standard.
  Teams now rarely allow run rate to drop below 7.5 RPO even when
  losing wickets — the modern approach is to maintain intent.
- **Bowling:** Spin-heavy overs 7–12. Captains bowl their best spinner
  in this window. Part-time spin from all-rounders is used to complete
  the over quota. Field spreads to cut off boundaries — long-on,
  long-off, deep midwicket.
- **Matchup manipulation:** Captains use left-arm spin against
  right-handed batters and off-spin against left-handers to create
  turning-away angles.

### Prediction implications

The middle-overs battle is where team composition matters most. A team
with three quality spinners on a turning pitch will dominate this phase.
Assess spin depth for both teams as a key prediction factor.

## Phase 3: Death Overs (Overs 16–20)

### Field restrictions

Same as middle overs (max five outside the ring). Captains typically
spread the field deep in this phase.

### Typical scoring patterns

- **Tournament-average run rate:** 10.5–12.0 RPO (highest of all phases)
- **Typical death-over runs:** 52–60 runs (over 5 overs)
- **Above average:** 65+ (finisher dominance or bowling collapse)
- **Below average:** Below 45 (excellent death bowling or wickets in hand
  issue)
- **Exceptional:** 75+ (rare — requires both batters set and poor bowling)

### Wicket patterns

- Average wickets lost: 2.0–3.0
- Wickets in overs 16–17 have less impact on final total than wickets in
  overs 18–20 (because there are still overs to rebuild).
- Teams with 6+ wickets in hand at the start of over 16 score 15–25 more
  runs in the death than teams with only 3–4 wickets in hand.

### Player types that dominate

- **Batters:** Designated finishers — power hitters with high strike
  rates in overs 16–20. The ability to hit sixes under pressure is more
  important than conventional technique. Finishers with SR 170+ in the
  death (career) are premium assets.
- **Bowlers:** Death-over specialists. Yorker accuracy is the single most
  important bowling skill. Economy in overs 16–20 below 9.0 is elite.
  Bowlers with reliable slower balls, wide yorkers, and the ability to
  execute under pressure. Left-arm pace with angled yorkers into
  right-handers' toes is particularly effective.

### Common tactics

- **Batting:** Maximum aggression. Target: 12+ RPO. Every ball is an
  opportunity to score. Running between wickets becomes critical —
  converting ones into twos can add 10–15 runs.
- **Bowling:** Yorkers or nothing. The back-of-length at the death is
  increasingly punished by modern batters. Wide yorkers outside off stump
  are the safest delivery. Slower-ball bouncers are high-risk/high-reward.
  Best death bowler typically bowls overs 17 and 19 (penultimate over is
  statistically the most important).
- **Field:** Deep square leg, deep fine leg, long-on, long-off are
  standard. Fielders on the boundary for catches off mistimed hits.

### Prediction implications

The death-overs differential is often the single biggest factor in match
outcomes. Compare death-over economy rates of both teams' best bowlers
and death-over strike rates of both teams' finishers.

## Second-Innings Chase Dynamics

### Run rate pressure

- **Overs 1–6:** Chasing teams typically score at a similar rate to
  the first innings powerplay if the required rate is under 9.0 RPO.
  If required rate exceeds 10.0 RPO from the start, batting becomes
  more aggressive and more reckless.
- **Overs 7–12:** This is where chases are won or lost. If the chasing
  team is ahead of the required rate, the probability of success rises
  sharply. If behind by more than 15 runs at the halfway mark, win
  probability drops below 30%.
- **Overs 13–16:** The "crunch point." The chasing team must decide
  whether to accelerate now or save wickets for the death. The required
  rate at the start of over 14 is the strongest in-game predictor of
  chase success.
- **Overs 17–20:** If required rate is below 10.0 with 5+ wickets in
  hand, chase succeeds ~75% of the time. Required rate above 13.0 with
  fewer than 5 wickets: chase succeeds <20%.

### When matches are won or lost in chases

- Most chases that succeed are effectively won by over 15 — the batting
  team has built a platform that makes the required rate manageable.
- Most chases that fail see the critical wicket cluster between overs
  8–14 — middle-order collapses under required-rate pressure.
- The "death-over heist" (needing 50+ off last 4 overs and winning) is
  dramatic but statistically rare: ~15–20% success rate.

### Historical chase success rates

- IPL-wide chase success rate: approximately 51–53% (slight advantage
  to chasing, driven by dew and known target).
- Chasing success rate increases to ~58% at venues with significant
  dew (Wankhede, Chinnaswamy, Delhi).
- Chasing success rate drops to ~45% on dry, deteriorating surfaces
  (Chepauk second match, Lucknow late season).

### Dew Factor

Dew is the single most important external condition affecting
second-innings outcomes in the IPL.

**How dew works:**
- After sunset (typically 7:00–7:30 PM local time), moisture settles
  on the grass outfield and sometimes on the pitch.
- The ball becomes wet and slippery, making it harder for bowlers to
  grip for spin, seam, or slower balls.
- Fielding deteriorates — misfields increase, the ball skids to the
  boundary faster on a wet outfield.

**Impact on bowling:**
- Spin effectiveness drops by 30–50% under heavy dew. Turn is reduced
  and the ball skids on.
- Seam bowlers lose grip for cutters and slower balls — their primary
  death-over weapons.
- Yorkers remain effective (dew has minimal impact on full-length
  deliveries) — this makes pure yorker bowlers more valuable in
  dew-affected matches.

**Impact on prediction:**
- At venues with strong dew, batting second is a meaningful advantage
  (5–8 percentage points shift from base rate).
- Toss decisions at dew venues are straightforward: the toss winner
  will choose to field first ~85% of the time.
- When a team bats first at a heavy-dew venue and posts 170, the
  chase is more achievable than the number suggests. Adjust par score
  downward by 10–15 runs.

**Dew onset timing:**
- Early-season matches (March–April): dew arrives later (8:30–9:00 PM)
  and is lighter.
- Mid-season (April–May): dew arrives earlier (7:30–8:00 PM) and is
  heavier.
- Late-season (May): Maximum dew at most venues. Second-innings
  bowling is significantly compromised.
