# Behavioral Pitfalls — Pre-Prediction Bias Checklist

**Run this checklist at the top of every prediction.md BEFORE forming
any probability estimates.** Answer each question honestly. If you flag
a bias, apply the corresponding correction before proceeding.

---

## Checklist

### 1. Desirability Bias

**Question:** Do I have a rooting interest in either team — as a fan,
because of a player I admire, or because a certain result would make a
better story?

**Cricket example:** You are predicting an RCB match and you find
yourself thinking "this could finally be their year." RCB's fan base is
enormous and their narrative of underperformance creates emotional pull
toward overrating their chances. Similarly, predicting a dominant team
like CSK or MI to win "feels right" because of their historical success
even when current form does not support it.

**Correction:** If you identify any rooting interest, anchor harder to
the market price. Do not allow your estimate to diverge from the market
in the direction of the team you favour unless you have a specific
informational reason.

---

### 2. Anchoring to a Single Data Point

**Question:** Am I anchoring to one recent result or one piece of news
rather than the full last-5-game picture?

**Cricket example:** Team A scored 230 in their last match and you are
now estimating a high first-innings total range. But their previous
four scores were 155, 168, 142, and 171. The 230 was an outlier on a
flat pitch, not a trend. Your score range should reflect all five
matches, not just the most memorable one.

**Correction:** Before forming any estimate, write down the full
last-5-game record for both teams. Base your initial estimate on the
full sample, not the most salient data point.

---

### 3. Over-Updating on Salient News

**Question:** Has a piece of breaking news (toss result, player injury,
pitch report) caused me to shift my estimate by more than the base rate
justifies?

**Cricket example:** The toss is won by Team A at a venue where the
toss winner has won 55% of matches historically. This justifies a shift
of roughly 5 percentage points from your pre-toss estimate — not 15.
Yet the psychological salience of the toss ("they got the conditions
they wanted!") often drives forecasters to over-update by 10–15 points.

**Correction:** Before adjusting for any single factor, look up the
historical base rate for that factor at this venue. Cap your adjustment
at the base rate effect size unless you have a specific reason this
match is different.

---

### 4. Confirmation Bias

**Question:** Have I formed an early opinion and then only sought
evidence that supports it?

**Cricket example:** You decide early that SRH will win because of
their bowling attack. You then spend time researching SRH's bowling
stats but do not equally examine the opposition's batting strengths
against pace, or SRH's own batting vulnerabilities. Your analysis
becomes a one-sided brief for SRH rather than a balanced assessment.

**Correction:** After forming your preliminary estimate, deliberately
spend equal time building the case for the other team. Use the Devil's
Advocate technique from forecasting-methods.md. If the opposing case
is credible, adjust.

---

### 5. Recency Bias / Trend Extrapolation

**Question:** Am I assuming a recent trend will continue without
checking whether it is statistically significant or just noise?

**Cricket example:** A batter has scored 15, 22, 8 in their last three
innings. You conclude they are "out of form." But their season average
is 35 with a strike rate of 145 — the last three innings are within
normal T20 variance. Three innings is not a meaningful sample for
declaring a form slump.

**Correction:** Always check the sample size. In T20 cricket, fewer
than 8–10 innings is too small to draw conclusions about a shift in
underlying ability. Weight the season-long or multi-season record more
heavily than the last 2–3 games.

---

### 6. Mood Contagion from External Sources

**Question:** Has social media sentiment, commentary hype, or a recent
result in an unrelated match influenced my thinking about this game?

**Cricket example:** You just watched an incredible last-over finish
in the previous match and are now in a heightened emotional state. You
unconsciously assign a higher probability to another close finish or
to the "exciting" team winning, when the fundamentals of this match
point to a more one-sided contest.

**Correction:** If you have been consuming live cricket or social media
commentary immediately before making a prediction, take a 5-minute
break. Re-read your conditions report and team analysis with fresh
eyes before forming your estimate.

---

## Summary: Three Mandatory Questions

Before writing ANY probability in prediction.md, answer these three
explicitly in writing:

1. **Do I have a rooting interest?** If yes → anchor harder to market.
2. **Am I anchoring to one recent event?** If yes → write out full
   last-5-game data first.
3. **Has salient news caused me to over-update?** If yes → check the
   base rate and cap adjustment accordingly.
