# The prediction market trader's edge

**The most important finding for anyone who has read *Superforecasting* and now trades on Kalshi or Polymarket is this: forecasting skill and trading skill are genuinely different capacities, and the evidence increasingly suggests that execution matters more than prediction.** A February 2026 study by Joshua Della Vedova decomposing 222 million Polymarket trades found that directional accuracy—knowing the right outcome—mattered less than entry price, timing, and position management. Meanwhile, a 2022 paper in the *International Journal of Forecasting* proved it is theoretically possible to profit systematically with a predictive model *less accurate* than the market's consensus. These findings challenge a core assumption many Tetlock-trained forecasters bring to prediction markets: that superior probability estimation automatically translates into superior returns.

This tension runs through every aspect of prediction market participation. The techniques that make someone a calibrated forecaster—base rate reasoning, Bayesian updating, humble probabilistic thinking—are necessary but insufficient for consistent profitability. Prediction markets demand a second layer of competence: understanding contract microstructure, managing liquidity risk, sizing positions for survival rather than just expected value, and maintaining psychological discipline when markets move against well-reasoned positions. What follows is an attempt to map both layers systematically—where they overlap, where they diverge, and where the evidence remains genuinely uncertain.

## PART 1 — PROBABILITY ESTIMATION TECHNIQUES

### 1. The forecaster's expanded toolkit

Philip Tetlock's work established five core techniques—base rate estimation, decomposition, inside/outside view, Bayesian updating, and extremizing—that lifted superforecasters above both casual predictors and credentialed experts. But top prediction market traders draw on a broader repertoire that extends into intelligence analysis, quantitative finance, and adversarial reasoning.

**Scenario weighting frameworks** formalize what decomposition begins informally. Rather than estimating a single probability, the trader constructs three to five discrete scenarios for how an event might unfold, assigns each a probability, and then derives the overall estimate as a weighted sum. The technique originated in corporate strategy—Royal Dutch Shell pioneered scenario planning in 1971—but its prediction market application is more granular. A trader evaluating "Will the Fed cut rates in June?" might construct scenarios around inflation trajectory, employment data, and geopolitical shocks, each with conditional probabilities. Aswath Damodaran's valuation framework requires four components: identifying critical factors, defining scenarios for each factor, estimating payoffs under each scenario, and assigning probabilities. The discipline of writing out scenarios forces traders to consider pathways they might otherwise neglect, particularly low-probability high-impact outcomes that prediction markets tend to misprice.

**Structured Analytic Techniques** from the intelligence community offer more formal debiasing methods. The most relevant is **Analysis of Competing Hypotheses** (ACH), developed by CIA veteran Richards Heuer in the 1970s and detailed in his freely available *Psychology of Intelligence Analysis*. ACH requires the analyst to list competing hypotheses, enumerate all available evidence, construct a diagnostic matrix plotting evidence against hypotheses, and focus on disconfirming rather than confirming evidence. Testing with intelligence analysts has produced mixed results—a 2024 review in *Intelligence and National Security* concluded ACH "has little to no overall benefit on judgment quality"—but the underlying principle of systematically evaluating competing explanations rather than anchoring on a preferred narrative is embedded in how top forecasters construct their rationales. Karvetski and colleagues found that top geopolitical forecasters' reasoning patterns naturally resemble ACH-style thinking even when they don't use the formal framework. Other SATs—**Key Assumptions Check**, **Red Team Analysis**, **Devil's Advocacy**—provide complementary structure for challenging one's own reasoning.

The **Kelly Criterion**, discussed at length in Part 3, functions as both a position-sizing tool and a probability estimation discipline. Because Kelly requires traders to specify their edge precisely—the gap between their estimated probability and the market price—it forces a level of calibration rigor that purely qualitative forecasting does not. A trader who cannot articulate why their estimate differs from the market price by a specific amount has no business placing a Kelly-sized bet.

**Adversarial collaboration**, a method championed by Tetlock through the Forecasting Research Institute (FRI), pairs individuals with opposing views on a question to jointly design research that could adjudicate their disagreement. The most striking application to date is the FRI's AI existential risk study, published in *Risk Analysis* in 2025: eleven "concerned" domain experts (median estimate: 20% chance of AI catastrophe by 2100) worked alongside eleven "skeptical" superforecasters (median estimate: 0.12%). Neither group changed their long-term estimates, but both pre-registered specific "cruxes"—empirical questions whose resolution by 2030 would genuinely update their views. For prediction market traders, the practical lesson is that identifying the specific empirical cruxes behind a disagreement is more productive than arguing about headline probabilities. Tetlock himself has noted the method's limitations: "My efforts at adversarial collaboration have not been all that successful. It's very hard to find the right dance partners."

**Extremizing aggregated forecasts** pushes averaged probabilities away from 50% to correct for the systematic moderation that occurs when you average diverse estimates. Baron, Mellers, Tetlock, and colleagues demonstrated in 2014 that this correction improves accuracy for two reasons: estimates near 0 and 1 are asymmetrically pulled toward 0.5, and individual forecasters dilute their personal confidence with their uncertainty about the best possible forecast. Satopää and colleagues formalized the optimal extremizing factor using a logit-based transformation, finding optimal values between **1.16 and 3.92** for geopolitical questions. A 2021 paper by Neyman and Roughgarden provided a principled formula for choosing the extremizing factor based on the number of forecasters, and testing on Metaculus data showed it outperformed all other aggregation methods. Whether to extremize prediction market prices specifically depends on whether the market has already incorporated sufficient information diversity—a genuinely open question.

**Confidence: High.** These techniques are well-documented in peer-reviewed literature and practitioner accounts. The main uncertainty is which techniques provide the largest marginal improvement for traders already versed in Tetlock's framework.

### 2. How domain shapes method

Prediction markets span categories with fundamentally different information structures, and professional forecasters adapt their methods accordingly. The same trader who uses polling averages for elections may use Poisson distributions for sports and machine learning classifiers for FDA approvals.

**Political event forecasting** centers on polling aggregation and error modeling. Nate Silver's methodology—now published through his Silver Bulletin Substack after FiveThirtyEight shut down in March 2025—involves adjusting polls for registered-versus-likely-voter screens, house effects, and pollster reliability ratings, then running Monte Carlo simulations incorporating correlated state-level errors. The key insight for prediction market traders is that polls are systematically biased in knowable ways: convention bounces average roughly **2.5 percentage points** and fade predictably, while the gap between registered and likely voter screens varies systematically by demographic composition. Silver's 2016 model gave Trump a 28.6% chance—substantially higher than competitors—precisely because it modeled correlated polling errors rather than treating state races as independent. For prediction market purposes, the PollyVote ensemble approach (combining polls, expert surveys, models, and market prices) has consistently outperformed any single method since its 2004 launch.

**Macroeconomic indicator forecasting** relies on established institutional infrastructure. The Philadelphia Fed's Survey of Professional Forecasters, Blue Chip Economic Indicators (consensus of ~50 economists), and Moody's Analytics scenario frameworks provide base rates. Kalshi now offers active markets on CPI, Fed rate decisions, GDP, and unemployment. The key domain-specific challenge is that macro data releases follow a strict calendar (CPI on the second Tuesday, FOMC meetings eight times annually), creating predictable windows of **price volatility and cross-platform spread widening** in the 48 hours before release. Smart traders position before these windows and exploit the post-release price adjustment period.

**Geopolitical conflict prediction** draws on quantitative early-warning systems. ACLED's Conflict Alert System (CAST) forecasts political violence events for every country up to six months ahead. Uppsala University's VIEWS system uses ensemble models with socioeconomic, geographic, and political variables to produce monthly global forecasts up to three years out. Mueller and Rauh (2022) demonstrated that topic modeling applied to millions of news articles, combined with conflict history variables, can predict organized violence onset at the country-month level. For prediction market traders, these systems provide base rates that are dramatically more informative than intuition. The challenge is that geopolitical markets on Polymarket and Kalshi tend to be less liquid and slower to incorporate information than political or sports markets.

**Sports markets** are the most liquid and fastest-adjusting category, accounting for **89% of Kalshi's 2025 fee revenue**. The dominant quantitative framework is the Elo rating system, originally designed for chess, now extended with margin-of-victory adjustments and logistic regression. FiveThirtyEight's NFL Elo model, mathematically equivalent to stochastic gradient descent on logistic regression, demonstrated the power of simple statistical models. Weighted Elo variants by Angelini and colleagues (2021) outperformed all competing methods on 60,000+ tennis matches. A critical finding for prediction market traders is that pre-match betting odds consistently outperform mathematical models, suggesting that the market itself has already incorporated most available statistical information. Edge in sports markets comes from speed of in-game information processing, not from building better pre-game models.

**Regulatory outcomes** present the most asymmetric information environment. Only **~12% of drugs entering Phase I** receive FDA approval, providing a strong base rate. Machine learning classifiers—Lo and Siah's 2019 study in *Harvard Data Science Review* used 5-nearest-neighbor random forests on drug compound characteristics and clinical trial data—can meaningfully predict approval odds. The RESOLVED2 model uses lasso-penalized Cox regression on Phase I oncology data with "FDA-approval-free survival" as its metric. For prediction market traders, the key advantage comes from deep domain expertise that the broader market lacks: understanding the significance of specific clinical trial endpoints, regulatory precedents, and FDA advisory committee dynamics.

**Confidence: High** for political and sports methods; **Medium** for macro and geopolitical (methods well-established but prediction market-specific applications less documented); **Medium** for regulatory (strong academic models but limited prediction market trading literature).

### 3. The calibration debate is more nuanced than either side admits

The question of whether prediction market prices are well-calibrated probability estimates has generated one of the field's most productive academic disagreements. The answer depends heavily on which platform, which market type, and which time horizon you examine.

The foundational debate pits **Charles Manski against Justin Wolfers and Eric Zitzewitz**. Manski argued in 2004 and 2006 that under risk-neutral traders with heterogeneous beliefs, the equilibrium price equals a quantile—not the mean—of the budget-weighted belief distribution, meaning prices only partially identify mean beliefs. Wolfers and Zitzewitz responded that Manski's result holds only under restrictive assumptions (risk-neutrality, fixed bet sizes) and that under more realistic conditions (log utility, variable bet sizes), prices closely approximate mean beliefs, with deviations typically limited to a few percentage points.

Recent empirical evidence has complicated both positions. **Reichenbach and Walther's December 2025 study**—analyzing **124 million trades** and roughly one million traders on Polymarket—found that market prices closely track realized probabilities and slightly outperform bookmaker odds. They found **no evidence of a general longshot bias on Polymarket**, contradicting the traditional finding. However, inaccuracies concentrated at two lifecycle points: early in a contract's life (insufficient information aggregation) and close to resolution (end-of-life pricing distortions).

Contrast this with **Bürgi, Deng, and Whelan's 2025-26 CEPR paper** analyzing transaction-level data on over **300,000 Kalshi contracts**. They found a clear favorite-longshot bias: contracts priced below 10¢ won far less than needed to break even, losing over 60% of invested capital, while contracts priced above 50¢ earned small positive returns. The average pre-fee return on a Kalshi contract was **minus 20%**. They distinguished between "Makers" (who post limit orders) and "Takers" (who accept them), finding both groups exhibited the bias but Takers lost substantially more—a form of winner's curse from preferring certainty of immediate execution.

The most damaging critique of market efficiency comes from **Clinton and Huang's December 2025 analysis** of $2.4 billion in 2024 presidential election trading across four platforms. They found that while 93% of PredictIt markets correctly predicted outcomes, accuracy was only 78% on Kalshi and 67% on Polymarket. More troublingly, prices for identical contracts diverged across platforms on 62 of 65 days before the election, daily price changes showed negative autocorrelation (suggesting overreaction followed by correction), and arbitrage opportunities peaked in the final two weeks. Their key finding: "A lot of the volatility was due to within-market actions and reactions"—traders responding to each other's trades rather than to new political information.

**Are these biases exploitable?** Snowberg and Wolfers (2010, *Journal of Political Economy*) demonstrated in horse racing that the favorite-longshot bias is explained by misperception of probabilities rather than risk-love—but found no profitable opportunities from betting on extreme favorites after accounting for transaction costs. Reade, Singleton, and Brown (2019, *International Journal of Forecasting*) found the opposite in Betfair football markets: a simple strategy exploiting underreaction to in-game goals yielded roughly **50% gross returns**, even out-of-sample. The exploitability question reduces to whether a specific market's transaction costs and liquidity permit capturing the bias before it corrects—a question with different answers on different platforms.

Eric Zitzewitz captured a structural irony in a 2025 interview: overconfidence is what makes prediction markets *function*. "You have to be a little bit overconfident to trade in a market that's inherently zero sum and then also charges you fees." Participants are disproportionately young, male, and educated. Overconfidence persists even after losses.

**Confidence: High** on the existence of biases; **Medium** on exploitability (varies dramatically by platform, market type, and fee structure). Practitioner consensus is weak—Polymarket's apparent absence of longshot bias directly contradicts Kalshi's documented presence of it, possibly reflecting different trader demographics.

### 4. The broader forecasting ecosystem

The Good Judgment Project established the paradigm, but several communities have developed distinct methodological contributions that prediction market traders should understand.

**Metaculus**, founded in 2015 by physicists Anthony Aguirre and Greg Laughlin, has become the most rigorous public forecasting platform, aggregating over **2.9 million predictions**. Its Community Prediction uses weighted medians giving more weight to forecasters with strong track records, and this aggregate performs as if ranked 4th on the entire platform—a striking demonstration of wisdom-of-crowds effects. Metaculus correctly predicted the Russian invasion of Ukraine two weeks in advance and assigned a 90% probability to Roe v. Wade being overturned months before the decision. Its distinctive contribution is AI forecasting benchmarking: in Q3 2024, 55 AI bots competed against 10 professional forecasters, with human pros still significantly outperforming the best bots (p=0.036), though the Mantic AI bot placed 8th out of 549 contestants—the first time an AI system cracked the top 10.

**Manifold Markets**, founded in December 2021 with funding from Scott Alexander's Astral Codex Ten grant program and $1.5 million from FTX Future Fund, pioneered user-created markets with play money ("Mana"). Its calibration claims forecasts within **4 percentage points of true probability** on average, and it outperformed other prediction market platforms in the 2022 midterms. The community contribution is speed and breadth—anyone can create a market on anything, producing a real-time probability layer over current events. Peter Bayley's analysis on LessWrong found that competent long-term traders accumulate Mana and dominate pricing, providing some manipulation resistance. However, Manifold's brief experiment with real-money "sweepcash" (September 2024 through March 2025) revealed the challenges of transitioning play-money insights to financial markets.

**Hypermind**, led by cognitive scientist Emile Servan-Schreiber (who co-authored the seminal 2004 paper "Prediction Markets: Does Money Matter?"), achieved near-perfect calibration over 8.5 years: **70% of events estimated at 70% probability actually occurred**, across 875,735 probability forecasts on 816 questions. In the 2014 US midterm elections, Hypermind matched or outperformed FiveThirtyEight and six other statistical models. Its continuous double auction with friction-free arbitrage algorithms represents one of the most technically sophisticated market mechanisms in the field. Corporate clients including EDF and Johns Hopkins Center for Health Security use Hypermind for operational forecasting.

**Quantitative traders** have transformed Polymarket into what one 2025 analyst described as "quietly becoming a quant battlefield where professional funds harvest edges the way they do in options and futures." The infrastructure supporting this includes dedicated VPS servers with sub-1-millisecond API latency, open-source market-making bots, and unified API wrappers like pmxt that normalize across platforms. Nate Silver now serves as advisor to Polymarket, bridging the forecasting and trading worlds. QuantPedia has published systematic analyses of prediction market edges covering inter-market and intra-market arbitrage, longshot bias exploitation, and behavioral bias patterns. The community's core framework is quantitative: logarithmic market scoring rules for modeling price impact, Kelly Criterion for sizing, Shannon entropy for information measurement, and correlation-based portfolio construction.

**Confidence: High.** These communities are well-documented and their performance claims are verifiable through public track records. The main gap is that community-specific methodologies are often tacit rather than formally published.

## PART 2 — CONTRACT AND MARKET ANALYSIS

### 5. Reading a contract like a professional

Sophisticated prediction market traders analyze contracts through a lens borrowed from market microstructure theory, treating the headline probability as just one signal among many.

**Order book imbalance** (OBI) is perhaps the most powerful signal beyond price itself. Defined as (Q_bid − Q_ask) / (Q_bid + Q_ask), OBI explains roughly **65% of short-interval price variance** according to Cont, Kukanov, and Stoikov (2014)—a finding from equity markets that applies directly to prediction market CLOBs. When bid-side depth substantially exceeds ask-side depth, prices tend to rise within 15–30 minutes with approximately 58% accuracy versus a 50% random baseline. The Volume-Adjusted Mid Price (VAMP), which cross-weights prices by opposite-side liquidity, provides a more reliable "true" price than the simple midpoint. Multi-level depth analysis (examining the order book 5 and 10 levels deep rather than just the best bid/offer) progressively improves predictive accuracy.

**Bid-ask spread dynamics** reveal market confidence and liquidity quality. Wider spreads signal lower liquidity, higher uncertainty, or thinner participation. On Kalshi, taker fees follow the formula ceil(0.07 × P × (1−P) × 100)/100, peaking at 50/50 odds at roughly 1.75% per contract and declining toward extreme probabilities. Polymarket Global charges no maker fees and only 2% of net profits at withdrawal, while Polymarket US charges a flat 0.10% taker fee. These fee asymmetries create structural spread differences across platforms that informed traders exploit.

**Volume spikes function as leading indicators** of information arrival. During the 2024 election, Polymarket trading surged sharply after each political shock—the Biden-Trump debate, the assassination attempt, Biden's withdrawal—particularly among incumbent traders with pre-event exposure. Information incorporation speed varies systematically by domain: sports markets process new information in under 5 minutes, political markets in **15–60 minutes**, and economic data markets in 30–180 minutes. An October 2025 example illustrates the extreme case: unusual betting activity on the Nobel Peace Prize market pushed the eventual winner's implied probability from roughly 3–4% to over 70% hours before the official announcement.

**Time decay analogues** in prediction markets operate differently from options theta but produce similar strategic implications. Page and Clemen (2013, *Economic Journal*) demonstrated that prediction markets are well-calibrated for short-horizon contracts but systematically biased toward 50% for distant events—explained by traders' reluctance to lock capital for long durations. As contracts approach settlement, gamma (the rate of price change acceleration) increases proportionally to 1/√(T_remaining), meaning small probability shifts cause exponentially larger price moves near resolution. Some professional traders manage this through position reduction formulas like Position(t) = Initial_Position × √(T_remaining / T_initial), systematically reducing exposure as settlement approaches.

**Information asymmetry detection** is uniquely possible on Polymarket due to on-chain transparency. Tools like PolyMonit and Ratio track wallets making trades above $10,000, enabling copy-trading and whale-following strategies. This transparency is unavailable on Kalshi's closed system. However, Columbia University research in November 2025 flagged approximately **14% of Polymarket wallets** with activity consistent with wash trading, accounting for 20–60% of volume in different periods—a significant caveat for anyone using raw volume data as an information signal.

**Confidence: High** for order book signals (well-supported by academic microstructure literature); **Medium** for time decay analogues (less formalized in prediction market contexts); **Low** for wash trading estimates (single study, methodological assumptions not yet widely scrutinized).

### 6. When prediction markets meet options theory

The analogy between prediction market contracts and financial options is mathematically precise in some respects and misleading in others. A prediction market contract paying $1 if an event occurs is formally equivalent to a **cash-or-nothing digital (binary) option**. The Greeks apply: delta measures sensitivity to the underlying probability driver, gamma rises sharply near settlement creating terminal risk concentration, theta manifests as gradual convergence toward $0 or $1, and vega captures sensitivity to uncertainty. The critical difference is that prediction markets have no continuous underlying price to delta-hedge against. Edge comes from estimating the true probability more accurately than consensus, not from hedging volatility.

**Spread strategies** are possible but informal. Traders exploit correlated contracts by constructing positions across related markets—for example, buying both "Fed raises rates in March" and selling "Inflation exceeds 3% in Q1" when the conditional probability relationship between these events appears mispriced. Kalshi explicitly supports "Combos" for expressing correlated opinions, such as linking weather forecasts to sports outcomes (cold weather → higher rushing attempts → specific game scoring patterns). On bracket markets for continuous variables like CPI, traders can construct vertical-spread-like positions by buying and selling adjacent brackets.

**Straddle and iron condor equivalents** are constrained by the binary settlement structure. Buying both YES and NO on the same contract is a guaranteed loss when prices sum to $1.00 or more—the structural opposite of a straddle. Volatility bets instead take the form of positions on correlated but distinct contracts where the trader expects a large move but is uncertain about direction. Cross-platform hedging—buying YES on one platform and NO on another when combined cost is below $1.00—constitutes the closest practical equivalent to a risk-free arbitrage, though execution risk is substantial.

**Cross-platform arbitrage** is real but highly constrained. Saguillo and colleagues (published at ACM AFT 2025) documented **$40 million in realized arbitrage profit** on Polymarket from April 2024 to April 2025 across 86 million bets. Roughly 60% came from simple binary complement arbitrage (YES + NO ≠ $1.00) and 40% from combinatorial arbitrage exploiting logical dependencies between markets. However, 62% of algorithmically detected combinatorial dependencies failed to yield profits, primarily due to liquidity asymmetry and non-atomic execution—orders cannot be guaranteed simultaneous fills across different markets.

Cross-platform arbitrage between Kalshi and Polymarket occurs roughly 5–15 times daily, with gaps typically lasting only **2–7 seconds**—dominated by bots. The biggest practical barrier is resolution rule divergence. In a government shutdown market, Polymarket required "OPM issues shutdown announcement" while Kalshi required "actual shutdown exceeding 24 hours." Holding opposite positions across platforms when resolution rules differ can result in total loss on both sides. Fee drag requires a minimum gross spread of roughly 2.0–2.5 cents for profitability.

A remarkable 2025 arXiv paper (2510.15205) proposes a unified framework—a "Risk-Neutral Jump-Diffusion" model—that serves as a prediction market equivalent of Black-Scholes, introducing a **belief-variance surface** analogous to the implied volatility surface. It defines event-linked derivatives including belief variance swaps, correlation swaps, and threshold notes, with Greeks defined with respect to probability and kernel parameters. This is the most ambitious attempt yet to create standardized pricing infrastructure for prediction markets.

Kris Abdelmessih's influential November 2025 analysis demonstrated how traditional options chains can identify mispriced prediction market contracts. Using vertical spread pricing, a trader can estimate the implied probability of a price level being breached and compare it to the prediction market price for the equivalent event. When Bitcoin traded at $89K, for example, the prediction market priced a 9% chance of hitting $250K by end of 2026—but options-derived estimates suggested roughly 5–6%, flagging the prediction market contract as potentially overpriced.

**Confidence: High** for the formal binary options analogy; **Medium** for practical options-like strategies (feasible but informal); **Low** for cross-platform arbitrage viability for retail traders (dominated by bots, resolution risk poorly understood).

### 7. How fast do prices learn?

The speed at which prediction market prices incorporate new information determines whether human traders can compete with algorithms—and the empirical evidence is category-dependent and sometimes surprising.

**Tsang and Yang's 2026 study** of Polymarket's 2024 presidential election market provides the most granular evidence. They decomposed transaction-level data around three major shocks. The Biden-Trump debate produced a price jump that **largely reversed**, suggesting initial overreaction driven by temporary liquidity pressure rather than durable belief updating. The assassination attempt on Trump produced a repricing that **persisted**, reflecting genuine belief change. Biden's withdrawal triggered two-sided trading with little net price change, reflecting genuine disagreement about implications. The first post-news price move, they found, is uninformative by itself. What matters is whether subsequent trading sustains or reverses the adjustment, and which types of traders supply the marginal order flow.

**Reade, Singleton, and Brown's 2019 study** in the *International Journal of Forecasting* produced a counterintuitive finding about poll releases on Intrade. Trading activity spiked immediately—but this activity was driven by *inexperienced* traders responding to the *existence* of a new poll rather than its content. Price efficiency actually **declined temporarily**, recovering only hours later when experienced traders entered. This creates a specific exploitable pattern: the initial reaction to scheduled information releases may make prices *worse*, not better, opening a window for informed traders.

Cross-platform price discovery during the 2024 election showed that Polymarket generally led Kalshi by minutes, attributable to higher liquidity and faster settlement. For financial markets, the comparison is humbling: equity markets adjust to scheduled macroeconomic news within **5 milliseconds**. Prediction markets operate on timescales of minutes to hours—dramatically slower, but still fast enough that manual trading strategies based on information speed face a narrow window.

The practical assessment for retail traders is mixed. The 48-hour window around scheduled announcements (CPI releases, Fed meetings) produces the most reliable cross-platform spreads and the widest price dislocations. Within-event price adjustment varies: sports in under 5 minutes, politics in 15–60 minutes, economics in 30–180 minutes. The realistic edge for human traders lies not in speed of information processing but in **superior interpretation**—understanding what a specific CPI print means for the Fed's reaction function better than the market does, and positioning before the consensus forms.

**Confidence: Medium.** The direction of evidence is clear (prediction markets are slower than financial markets, overreact to salient news, and show category-dependent adjustment speeds), but few studies have rigorously tested out-of-sample profitability of specific exploitation strategies.

### 8. Three platforms, three strategic universes

The structural differences between Kalshi, Polymarket, and PredictIt create meaningfully different strategic environments, and strategies that work on one platform may fail on another.

**Kalshi** operates as a CFTC-designated contract market since 2020, with election markets approved after a September 2024 federal court ruling and sports markets launched in January 2025. Its fee structure uses the formula ceil(0.07 × P × (1−P) × 100)/100, peaking at about 1.75% at 50/50 odds. Maker fees were introduced in mid-2025 at roughly 25% of taker rates. Kalshi processed approximately **$22.88 billion in volume in 2025**, raised $1 billion at an $11 billion valuation in December 2025, and integrated with Robinhood in March 2025. Sports now dominate: 89% of fee revenue. Resolution is centralized through an internal markets team referencing named "Source Agencies" filed with the CFTC. The March 2026 Khamenei contract dispute—where Kalshi invoked a "death carve-out" clause and settled at last traded price rather than paying YES holders—illustrates the resolution risks of centralized determination.

**Polymarket** has undergone a dramatic regulatory transformation. After a 2022 CFTC fine for operating an unregistered derivatives platform, it blocked US users until December 2025, when it launched Polymarket US through its $112 million acquisition of CFTC-licensed exchange QCEX. The international version operates on Polygon blockchain with USDC settlement, using a hybrid Central Limit Order Book (replacing its original AMM) with off-chain matching and on-chain settlement. Polymarket Global charges **0% trading fees** on most markets with 2% of net profits at withdrawal; Polymarket US charges 0.10% taker fee with no maker fee. Resolution uses UMA's Optimistic Oracle: anyone can propose an outcome by posting a $750 bond, with a 2-hour challenge window and escalation to UMA token holder voting if disputed. Approximately 98.5% of markets resolve without reaching the voting stage. ICE (NYSE owner) invested up to $2 billion at an $8–9 billion valuation.

**PredictIt** survived its near-death experience. After the CFTC withdrew its 2014 no-action letter in August 2022, PredictIt sued in the Western District of Texas, won Fifth Circuit injunctive relief, and ultimately prevailed in July 2025 when the court declared the CFTC's closure efforts invalid. Under updated terms, the $850 position limit rose to **$3,500** and the 5,000-trader-per-market cap was eliminated. PredictIt won DCM approval in September 2025. Its fees remain the industry's highest: 10% of gross profits plus 5% withdrawal fee, creating a combined effective rate that can exceed 14%. This fee structure makes small edges unprofitable and explains why PredictIt markets historically showed the most persistent mispricings—the fees made arbitrage economically nonviable.

The strategic implications are substantial. Kalshi's fee structure favors high-probability contracts (where fees are lowest) and penalizes 50/50 bets. Polymarket Global's zero-fee structure enables strategies—like high-frequency market making and arbitrage—that would be fee-prohibitive elsewhere. PredictIt's high fees create an environment where only very large edges (above ~15%) are worth trading. Resolution rule differences across platforms create the single largest risk for cross-platform arbitrageurs. And liquidity profiles differ markedly: Kalshi's sports markets are deep while its political markets are thinner; Polymarket has historically led in political market depth but lags in sports.

**Confidence: High.** Platform structures are publicly documented and independently verifiable. The main uncertainty is regulatory trajectory—all three platforms face evolving state and federal regulatory landscapes that could change fee structures, market availability, and access rules.

## PART 3 — PORTFOLIO AND RISK MANAGEMENT

### 9. Thinking in portfolio terms is rare but increasingly necessary

Most prediction market participants trade individual contracts in isolation. The evidence suggests that the minority who think in portfolio terms have a meaningful advantage, but formal frameworks remain underdeveloped.

The best-documented example of portfolio-level thinking comes from **Domer (@ImJustKen on Polymarket)**, one of the platform's most successful traders with over $2.5 million in net profit across roughly 10,000 predictions. In a January 2026 interview, Domer described going through hundreds of markets daily and maintaining simultaneous positions across categories: "I have positions open on everything from strikes on Iran, and Fed Chair position, to Avatar and who will win the Oscar for best picture." Of his 10,000 predictions, approximately 8,000 were small positions—a pattern consistent with portfolio diversification rather than concentrated conviction betting.

Practitioner guides consistently recommend spreading capital across different time horizons, event categories, risk levels, and market structures. The specific warning about correlation risk is explicit in community writing: "If your entire book depends on one election cycle, one regulatory outcome, or one public figure, one surprise can hit every position at once." Copy-trading frameworks suggest allocating across grinder wallets (consistent small-edge traders), specialist wallets (domain experts), and whale wallets (high-conviction concentrated bets), with an explicit rule to "reduce size when multiple wallets converge on the same trade."

Position sizing rules from practitioners include never risking more than **5% of total portfolio on a single trade**, maintaining minimum portfolios of $500–$1,000 for adequate diversification across 5–10 positions, and monitoring for correlated positions that should be treated as a single exposure.

The most sophisticated portfolio thinking connects prediction markets to traditional financial instruments. Practitioners note correlations between prediction market outcomes and financial assets—election outcomes correlate with VIX, currency pairs (USD/MXN showed ~0.42 correlation with 2024 election outcomes), and Treasury yields. This opens the possibility of cross-asset hedging: holding a prediction market position while using options on a correlated ETF as insurance against the adverse outcome.

The significant gap is the absence of any academic literature on prediction market portfolio construction from the participant's perspective. The institutional risk management platform TS Imagine has noted that event contracts "make explicit risks that have always been present but obliquely expressed," suggesting formal institutional portfolio integration is coming—but it has not arrived.

**Confidence: Medium.** Practitioner evidence is consistent but informal. No academic treatment of optimal portfolio construction across prediction market positions exists. The evidence for cross-asset hedging is suggestive but not rigorously tested.

### 10. Kelly in practice: consensus, controversy, and failure modes

The Kelly Criterion provides the most natural bridge between forecasting skill and position sizing, but its prediction market application involves genuine expert disagreement and documented pitfalls.

The standard Kelly formula for binary prediction markets is straightforward: for a contract priced at π with your estimated true probability p, the optimal fraction of bankroll to risk is **f* = (p − π) / (1 − π)** for buying YES contracts. Bernhard Meister's December 2024 arXiv paper formalizing Kelly for prediction markets emphasized a crucial point that platforms often obscure: prediction market prices do not equal true probabilities. The gap arises from risk aversion, fee structures, and asymmetric payoff structures. Meister used Kullback-Leibler divergence to analyze how misjudging the true probability or miscalculating the investment fraction reduces portfolio growth rate.

**The strong consensus favoring fractional Kelly** is one of the clearest points of agreement in the practitioner literature. UC Berkeley's David Aldous described full Kelly as "the boundary between aggressive and insane investing"—more aggressive than Kelly guarantees lower growth rates and can turn growth negative. A study where subjects faced a 60/40 biased coin found that **28% went bust** and only 21% reached the maximum payout, demonstrating humans' poor intuitive grasp of optimal betting. Half-Kelly is the most commonly recommended fraction, with some practitioners preferring quarter-Kelly for additional safety margin. A concrete example: estimating 60% true probability on a contract priced at 45¢, the edge is 0.15, full Kelly suggests risking 27% of bankroll, but the "pro approach" per practitioner guides is half-Kelly at 13.5% maximum.

A key theoretical insight from Beygelzimer and colleagues at UMass reframes fractional Kelly as **confidence weighting**: when λ < 1, the agent effectively uses a belief that is a weighted average of (1−λ) × market_price + λ × own_belief. This provides a principled justification beyond ad hoc risk reduction—fractional Kelly is mathematically equivalent to hedging your probability estimate toward the market's estimate, which is exactly what a Bayesian agent with finite confidence in their own assessment should do.

**Kelly with correlated bets** remains a significant gap. Rising and Wyner showed that fractional Kelly portfolios equal full Kelly portfolios using shrinkage estimators, linking fractional Kelly to statistical estimation theory. Frontiers in Applied Mathematics published work on incorporating Kelly into Markowitz-style portfolio optimization for correlated assets. But no published framework optimally handles the specific correlation structure of prediction markets—where multiple election markets, for instance, resolve simultaneously and are driven by common factors.

A bachelor's thesis on Kelly in market-scoring-rule markets identified a critical technical problem: in automated market maker-based markets, **the act of trading moves the price**, so naive Kelly calculations lead to overbetting. A "non-naive" Kelly application anticipates the price impact and adjusts downward, while a "capped" approach limits the maximum trade size. The capped approach generally outperformed non-naive Kelly in simulations but carried higher ruin risk.

**Documented failure modes** include parameter estimation error (the dominant practical risk—overestimating your edge leads to overbetting and potential ruin), non-stationarity (real-world probabilities change but Kelly assumes stable parameters), excessive drawdowns under full Kelly (psychologically intolerable for most traders), and the absence of time-value consideration (Kelly doesn't account for the fact that shares closer to maturity are more valuable).

**Confidence: High** for fractional Kelly superiority over full Kelly; **Medium** for specific fraction recommendations (half-Kelly is convention, not optimization); **Low** for Kelly with correlated prediction market bets (genuine gap in literature).

### 11. The mind as the weakest instrument

Prediction market trading generates psychological pitfalls that go beyond generic cognitive biases, because the market structure itself creates unique behavioral traps.

**Anchoring to opening contract prices** is among the most insidious. Fox and Clemen (2005) and Sonnemann and colleagues (2013) demonstrated that traders unconsciously anchor toward equal distribution across categories—a 1/N bias—meaning that market designers can inadvertently bias prices through how they structure outcome bins. When a contract opens at 50¢, subsequent trading shows persistent pull toward that anchor even as information accumulates. Practitioner guides emphasize creating "baseline pricing" independently before looking at market prices, but this requires unusual discipline.

**Over-updating on salient news** creates predictable overreaction patterns. Markets take 3–15 minutes to fully price breaking news, during which early movers capture 20–50% of the eventual price movement. But as Tsang and Yang (2026) documented, the initial direction of movement is not always sustained—the Biden-Trump debate price jump largely reversed, suggesting the first movers were responding to salience rather than information content. The practitioner heuristic "buy panic, sell relief" codifies the contrarian response to salient-news overreaction.

**The illiquidity trap** is structurally specific to prediction markets. Unlike equity positions that can almost always be sold (at some price), prediction market contracts in thin markets may have no buyer at any reasonable price. Page and Clemen found that prices for distant events are systematically biased toward 50% partly because traders have time preferences against locking capital for extended periods. A trader who has identified genuine edge on a six-month contract may be unable to exit the position if their thesis changes—the market simply lacks opposing flow. This is compounded by the absence of dynamic margining: capital is fully committed at entry with no mechanism for partial release.

**Optimism bias in domain-specific markets** was documented rigorously by Cowgill and Zitzewitz (2015) in Google's internal prediction markets. They found that markets consistently overestimated on-time project delivery, especially when outcomes were under employees' control. Insiders and their friends traded optimistically. New hires were the most optimistic; calibration improved with organizational experience. In public prediction markets, an analogous effect appears as **desirability bias**: Park and colleagues (2023) found that people "express more bias in their predictions than in their likelihood judgments"—they predict outcomes they want to happen, a particularly potent bias in political markets where identity and prediction intertwine.

**Mood contagion from correlated assets** creates a subtle bias. Google's internal markets showed that a 2% increase in Google's stock price was associated with prediction market prices being **3–4 percentage points higher** for optimistic outcomes—an effect that was temporary (disappearing after two days) but real during trading hours. For Polymarket and Kalshi traders who also follow crypto or equity markets, bullish sentiment in their primary portfolio may unconsciously inflate their probability estimates on prediction market contracts.

**Confirmation bias and partisan trading** particularly distort political markets. The "fade the crowd" strategy explicitly targets emotionally driven markets: when retail traders flood a contract due to social media hype, contrarian positions become profitable. The Wall Street Journal's report of four Polymarket accounts wagering ~$30 million on Trump that behaved identically illustrates the extreme case—but even without coordination, partisan enthusiasm systematically moves prices away from calibrated estimates.

**Confidence: High** for anchoring, overreaction, and desirability bias (well-documented academically); **Medium** for illiquidity trap and mood contagion (documented but less rigorously studied in prediction market-specific contexts).

### 12. Where to go from here

The landscape of prediction market-specific educational resources is thin compared to traditional finance but growing rapidly. Distinguishing rigorous material from marketing content requires careful evaluation.

The foundational academic texts remain indispensable. Tetlock's *Superforecasting* (2015) provides the forecasting methodology. Nate Silver's *On the Edge* (2024) bridges forecasting, poker, sports betting, and prediction markets more directly than his earlier *Signal and the Noise*. Richards Heuer's *Psychology of Intelligence Analysis* is freely available on the CIA website and provides the SAT framework. Pherson and Heuer's *Structured Analytic Techniques for Intelligence Analysis* is the comprehensive reference for the 27+ formal debiasing methods. Emile Servan-Schreiber's *Supercollectif* addresses collective intelligence from the Hypermind founder's perspective.

The most important academic papers for prediction market traders specifically are Wolfers and Zitzewitz (2006) on interpreting prices as probabilities; Snowberg and Wolfers (2010) on the favorite-longshot bias mechanism; Baron et al. (2014) on extremizing; Bürgi, Deng, and Whelan (2025–26) on Kalshi maker/taker dynamics; Reichenbach and Walther (2025) on Polymarket calibration; Clinton and Huang (2025) on cross-platform efficiency; Meister (2024) on Kelly in prediction markets; and Saguillo et al. (2025) on arbitrage opportunities. The Della Vedova (2026) paper on execution versus information is potentially the most important for practitioners transitioning from forecasting to trading.

Practitioner resources have proliferated. **Silver Bulletin** (Nate Silver's Substack) provides ongoing forecasting and prediction market commentary from someone now advising Polymarket. **Astral Codex Ten** (Scott Alexander's blog) runs annual prediction contests comparing forecasters and platforms. **LessWrong** and the **Effective Altruism Forum** host active discussions on forecasting methodology, AI forecasting bots, and calibration analysis. The **Forecasting Research Institute** publishes research on adversarial collaboration and existential risk forecasting. **QuantPedia** has published "Systematic Edges in Prediction Markets" covering arbitrage strategies and bias exploitation.

For training, **Good Judgment Inc.** offers a self-paced online course on Superforecasting fundamentals (probabilistic thinking, calibration, cognitive biases, Fermi estimation) with certification. **Good Judgment Open** provides a free public forecasting tournament for practice. Metaculus's quarterly tournaments with cash prizes offer competitive forecasting practice with AI benchmarking. No course specifically designed around prediction market *trading* strategy (as distinct from forecasting) exists in any formal institutional setting—this is a genuine gap. The closest approximation is the practitioner guide ecosystem: NYC Servers' market-making guide, various Substack analyses of quantitative strategies, and GitHub repositories including Polymarket market-maker bots and Metaculus's open-source forecasting-tools framework.

**CalibrationCity** allows direct comparison of calibration across Metaculus, Manifold, and other platforms. **0xinsider.com** tracks large trades on Polymarket and Kalshi in real time. **EventArb.com** and **ArbBets** provide cross-platform arbitrage calculators.

The **Manifest conference**, hosted annually in Berkeley by Manifold Markets, has featured Nate Silver, Robin Hanson, and Eliezer Yudkowsky—the closest the field has to an industry gathering.

**Confidence: High** for the resource landscape as described; **Medium** for the quality assessment of practitioner resources (survivorship bias in trading guides is rampant, and claimed performance metrics should be treated skeptically).

### 13. The forecaster and the trader are not the same person

The question of what separates a competent Tetlock-style forecaster from a skilled prediction market trader has received surprisingly little direct academic attention, but the evidence that exists points toward a nuanced answer: the skills are **partially overlapping, genuinely additive, and occasionally in tension**.

The strongest evidence that forecasting skill translates to trading comes from the IARPA tournaments themselves, where Tetlock's superforecasters beat prediction markets by **15–30%** on Brier scores. This suggests that elite forecasting skill can identify mispricings that markets fail to correct—at least in the tournament's specific structure, where forecasters faced no execution costs, liquidity constraints, or capital management requirements.

The strongest evidence that forecasting skill is *insufficient* comes from Della Vedova's February 2026 paper, "Who Profits from Prediction Markets? Execution, not Information," decomposing 222 million Polymarket trades into directional (prediction) and execution (trading) components. The title delivers the verdict: across the full trader population, execution quality—entry price relative to final value—was more important than directional accuracy for profitability. A complementary theoretical result from Šourek and colleagues (2022, *International Journal of Forecasting*) proved that it is possible to make systematic profits with a predictive model *less accurate than the market*, by decorrelating the model from market prices and exploiting structural biases in market-maker pricing.

The CFA Institute's review of *Superforecasting* captured the tension precisely: "The forecasting described in the book does not rise (or fall) to the level of measuring whether good forecasting is exploitable in an investment sense." Superforecaster traits—probabilistic thinking, frequent updating, intellectual humility, foxlike eclecticism—differ from trader traits: execution timing, risk management discipline, emotional control under uncertainty, contract interpretation expertise, and platform-specific operational knowledge. The foxy eclecticism that makes superforecasters excellent at considering multiple perspectives may actually conflict with the decisiveness required for trade execution.

Only roughly **16.8% of Polymarket wallets** show net gains, a statistic that strongly suggests forecasting skill alone is insufficient without trading discipline. The market's zero-sum-minus-fees structure means that most participants—many of whom presumably believe they have good judgment—lose money. The edge that separates winners from losers appears to be a combination of calibrated probability estimates *and* execution quality: entering positions at favorable prices, sizing appropriately for survival rather than maximum expected value, managing correlated exposures, and maintaining psychological discipline during adverse moves.

Some practitioners argue the skills are more than additive—that the discipline of trading (where miscalibration costs real money) actually improves forecasting. The Google internal market studies support this: traders whose probability estimates were tested against outcomes showed calibration improvements over time. Others argue there is genuine tension: a forecaster who correctly identifies a 55% probability on a contract priced at 50¢ faces a tiny edge that Kelly would size conservatively, yet the psychological reward of "being right" may tempt them to oversize the position.

**Confidence: Medium.** The theoretical arguments are well-developed, but empirical evidence specifically comparing forecasting skill and trading skill in matched samples is thin. The Della Vedova finding is striking but based on a single platform's data. Practitioner consensus is weak—some veteran traders dismiss forecasting theory entirely while others consider it foundational.

## CONCLUSION — Toward a unified framework that doesn't yet exist

The evidence assembled across these thirteen questions points toward a conclusion that should be both humbling and energizing for anyone trained in Tetlock's tradition who now trades prediction markets: **no unified framework for prediction market participation exists, and the reason it doesn't exist is that the problem is genuinely harder than either the forecasting community or the trading community has acknowledged.**

The forecasting tradition, running from Tetlock through the Good Judgment Project to Metaculus and the Forecasting Research Institute, has produced remarkable tools for probability estimation. Base rate reasoning, decomposition, the inside/outside view, Bayesian updating, and extremizing aggregation demonstrably improve calibration. SATs from intelligence analysis provide structured debiasing. Adversarial collaboration identifies the cruxes of genuine disagreement. These techniques work. Superforecasters beat prediction markets by 15–30% in controlled tournaments, and the optimally extremized aggregate of skilled forecasters has consistently outperformed individual methods.

But the trading tradition reveals that accurate probability estimation is a necessary yet insufficient condition for profitability. The Della Vedova finding—execution, not information—should land with force. The Šourek result—profiting with inferior predictions by decorrelating from market consensus—should provoke deeper thought. The Kelly Criterion literature tells us that sizing matters as much as direction, and that the optimal sizing strategy depends on correlated exposures that no published framework handles adequately for prediction markets. The platform analysis reveals that fee structures, resolution rules, and liquidity profiles create strategic environments so different that a single approach cannot work across Kalshi, Polymarket, and PredictIt.

The biases documented in Part 1—longshot bias on Kalshi but not Polymarket, overreaction to salient news, anchor effects from opening prices—represent both exploitable opportunities and psychological hazards. A trader who understands these biases has edge. A trader who understands them but cannot execute in the 15–60 minute window before markets correct does not. A trader who can execute but bases positions on uncalibrated probability estimates will size incorrectly and eventually face ruin through the compounding asymmetry that Kelly identifies: overbetting destroys wealth faster than underbetting forfeits opportunity.

The argument across these four parts suggests that forecasting skill and trading skill are **additive but operate on different timescales and different failure modes**. Forecasting skill operates on the timescale of probability estimation—getting the numbers right before entering a position. Trading skill operates on the timescale of execution—entering at favorable prices, managing position size relative to edge confidence, monitoring for correlation risk, and maintaining psychological discipline through adverse variance. The two skills rarely conflict directly, but they compete for attention and mental resources: the same trader cannot simultaneously be updating Bayesian priors on sixteen geopolitical scenarios and monitoring order book depth for entry signals.

The closest approximation to a unified framework would integrate Tetlock's probability estimation methodology (the "CHAMP" framework from *Superforecasting*—Comparison classes, Historical trends, Averaging, Mathematical models, Present information) with Kelly-based position sizing informed by calibration confidence, portfolio-level correlation management drawn from quantitative finance, platform-specific execution strategies adapted to fee structures and liquidity profiles, and systematic psychological discipline against the specific biases that prediction market structures create. No published work integrates all five of these components. The Beygelzimer insight—that fractional Kelly is mathematically equivalent to hedging one's probability estimate toward the market's estimate—provides the most elegant theoretical bridge, connecting forecasting confidence directly to trading strategy through a single parameter.

The field is at an inflection point. Prediction market combined monthly volume across platforms approaches **$10 billion** as of late 2025. Professional market makers and quantitative firms have entered with infrastructure, capital, and speed that casual participants cannot match. The $40 million in documented Polymarket arbitrage profits shows that structural edges exist and are being harvested at scale. Yet only 16.8% of wallets show net gains, and the persistent divergence of prices for identical contracts across platforms during the 2024 election demonstrates that even "efficient" markets leave substantial room for disciplined participants.

The unified framework that prediction market participants need will emerge from the intersection of two traditions that have historically developed in isolation. The forecasting community has the better theory of probability estimation. The trading community has the better theory of execution and risk management. The prediction market participant who can hold both frameworks simultaneously—calibrating probabilities with Tetlock's rigor and executing with a trader's discipline—occupies a position that neither tradition alone has optimized for. The evidence suggests this synthesis is possible but rare. It demands not just intellectual range but the psychological capacity to maintain probabilistic humility (the forecaster's virtue) while acting decisively under uncertainty (the trader's virtue). These are not contradictory demands, but they are demanding in combination, and the scarcity of participants who master both may be the most durable edge in the prediction market ecosystem.
