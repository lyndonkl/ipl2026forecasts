## PREDICTION FAILURES — ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS

### Failure 1: Sanju Samson Form Dip Assumption (-18% SR)

**What we predicted:** Samson would underperform, averaging ~7.5 runs off 7 balls in PP, following games 003 and 011 pattern.

**What actually happened:** Samson scored 115* off 56 balls (SR 205), including 15 fours and 4 sixes. He was the dominant player of the match.

**Root cause:** 
1. **Two-game sample bias:** We over-weighted a 2-game form dip (G003: 6 off 7, G011: 9 off 7) as a "signal" of form collapse.
2. **Transition completion underestimated:** Samson's transition to CSK was incomplete in games 003 and 011 (first 2 games for him at CSK), but by Game 018 (his 3rd game for CSK after April 3), he had settled in and was ready to explode.
3. **Career baseline ignored:** Samson's T20 career SR is 135+, and he has 3x 50+ scores already in IPL career. A 2-game dip does not negate career form; regression to mean was the higher-probability outcome.
4. **Home advantage discount:** Playing at Chepauk (CSK's home), Samson faced familiar bowlers and pitch conditions he could adapt to faster.

**Forecastability:** THIS WAS FORECASTABLE. We should have recognized:
- 2-game sample is noise (Noise classification, not Signal)
- CSK home advantage for overseas player learning curve
- Chepauk drying pitch favors aggressive batting (contra our assumption that turns favor spinners, spinners do favor bowlers, but aggressive batting on drying pitches can override spin)
- Market pricing CSK 45% (vs our 35%) suggested market was already pricing in Samson's potential

**Lesson:** Avoid over-weighting recent negative games on small samples (2 games). Use 5-game windows or classify as "Noise" explicitly. When career baseline is 135 SR and 2 games show 60-90 SR, default assumption should be regression to mean, not continuation of dip.

### Failure 2: CSK Spin Dominance Assumption (Kuldeep > Chahar)

**What we predicted:** Kuldeep Yadav's elite wrist-spin on turning Chepauk pitch would dominate CSK's Chahar + Hosein, driving LR 0.75 (strongly favoring DC bowling in EM phase).

**What actually happened:** 
1. CSK's EM bowling was never tested — Samson and Short were so dominant that they weren't present long enough for spinners to dominate.
2. Kuldeep did get early EM wickets in DC's chase (elite performance confirmed), but CSK batting never faced the full "EM spin choke" scenario we modeled.

**Root cause:** 
1. **Scenario dependency:** Our LR 0.75 was calculated for a "CSK 165-172 total, Brevis-less team facing Kuldeep" scenario. But CSK actually scored 212 with Samson dominant, meaning Kuldeep faced DC chase (not CSK batting) in EM phase where his elite threat did materialize.
2. **Conditional probability error:** We treated EM phase probabilities as independent of PP outcomes, but they're not. A strong CSK PP (60 runs vs predicted 45-52) changes the entire EM dynamic for DC's chase.
3. **Underestimated Samson impact on bowling:** When a batter is scoring 10+ RPO off an elite spinner (Kuldeep), it's not because Kuldeep is weak — it's because the batter is elite. We didn't account for Samson's sheer batting dominance offsetting Kuldeep's quality.

**Forecastability:** PARTIALLY FORECASTABLE. 
- Kuldeep's elite threat was real and materialized in DC's chase (7 of Kuldeep's 3 wkts likely in EM/LM phases of chase).
- But Samson's counter-dominance was a 2-game sample issue (noise), making it reasonable to forecast spin choke if Samson continued his dip. We were right about Kuldeep being elite; we were wrong about Samson being weak.

**Lesson:** When decomposing LR by player matchups, validate the base case (expected score distribution) against recent player form spikes. A FORM SURGE flag (Samson -18% → actual +205%) should prompt scenario recalculation even if the spin dominance micro-factor is correct.

**Forecastability:** Dhoni's absence was correctly identified and accounted for. The failure was not in death-overs LR, but in under-weighting Samson's upside earlier in the innings.

**Lesson:** Identify which phases determine match outcome given likely score distributions. If Samson's form dip was noise (which it was), then CSK's pre-death innings control was the determining factor, not death-overs finisher strength.

### Failure 5: DC Bullish Ceiling Overweighted

**What we predicted:** DC's elite openers (Rahul +15%, Nissanka +39% SR) would dominate CSK's weak new-ball threat. DC's probability of winning was 65% (Bullish 38%, Neutral 38%, Bearish 24%).

**What actually happened:** DC's PP was Bullish (61 runs in 5 overs), confirming our prediction. But DC collapsed in EM phase (overs 7-12) with 3-4 wickets lost, ending any chase hopes by over 12-13.

**Root cause:**
1. **Underestimated Kuldeep's impact on DC batting:** In DC's chase, Kuldeep faced Rana/Stubbs (weaker middle-order players) rather than elite batter. His elite threat materialized fully. We had Kuldeep elite in our hand, but we modeled EM phase (7-12) as Neutral (1.35 LR) rather than Bearish for chase context.
2. **Chase dynamics vs bat-first dynamics:** In a bat-first scenario, EM phase LR 1.35 (batter edge) is reasonable for set batters (Stubbs/Miller quality). But in a chase (where required rate pressure compounds bowler advantage), the same 1.35 LR is too bullish. We didn't distinguish chase-context LR from bat-first-context LR.

**Forecastability:** THIS WAS FORECASTABLE but required chase-context recalibration.
- Kuldeep's elite threat was real (3 wkts in 3 games pre-match).
- Rana's UNKNOWN CEILING was documented (we estimated 5-45 runs, but didn't lean into the "5 runs" tail risk).
- CSK's EM bowling wasn't tested, so Chahar's unproven status vs Kuldeep elite should have driven scenario B bullish weighting down, which would have warned us about Scenario A (CSK bat first, DC chase) being more Bearish.

**Lesson:** Distinguish phase LRs between bat-first and chase contexts. A 1.35 LR (batter edge) in EM phase for bat-first (CSK 1st inn) is different from 1.35 LR in EM phase for chase (DC 2nd inn) given pressure, required rate, and wicket cluster risk.

## FORWARD-LOOKING LESSONS FOR CALIBRATION

1. **Form dip sample size threshold:** Never classify a 2-game form dip as "Signal." Use 5-game minimum for Signal classification, or explicitly weight 2-game observations at 20-30% vs career mean (default 70-80% weight on career baseline).

2. **Player dominance vs form regression:** When a player has career baseline 135+ SR and recent dip to 60-90 SR (2 games), the default model should project reversion to ~130 SR, not continuation of dip. Samson's actual 205 SR is extreme, but 120-140 SR (reversion range) would have eliminated the DC 65% edge entirely.

3. **Chase context LR adjustment:** Discount chase phase LRs by 0.05-0.15 relative to bat-first LRs for the same match context. Wicket cluster risk (2+ wickets in 2-3 overs) is higher in chase when required rate is 10+ RPO. Example: EM LR 1.35 (bat-first) → 1.20 (chase context, same bowlers/batters).

4. **Unknown ceiling volatility in chase:** When a batter has UNKNOWN CEILING (e.g., Rana P25-P75 5-45), weight the P25 (5 runs) more heavily in chase scenarios than in bat-first scenarios. Pressure + unfamiliar opposition = higher tail risk on lower end.

5. **Bankroll sizing on model sensitivity:** When an edge is heavily dependent on 1-2 player assumptions (Samson form dip drove 30pp of the 35pp edge), lower the Kelly sizing fraction. Samson 2-game sample should have prompted "Lower Kelly size due to concentration of edge in 1-2 unknowns" → moved from Half Kelly (12.8%) to Quarter Kelly (6.4%).

6. **Post-toss recalibration trigger:** Even if lock happens pre-toss, when toss outcome is known, implement a mandatory +/-5% recalibration check on player-dependent edges. Example: Samson opening in familiar CSK XI (vs predicted newcomer anxiousness) → +3-5% recalibration on his form upside post-toss.
